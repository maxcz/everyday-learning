*Inspiration/Source: Book <a href="https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/48528524-awareness" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Giant Steps</a> by Tony Robbins, lived experience* 

I have an hour before I meet friends. I open the address, I start working out the route, and I spot the name of a bar on the way. The name reminds me of a place I went to in India.
 
So I look it up. The bar leads to the town, the town leads to a memory, and the memory leads to me in satellite mode on Google Maps, dragging across a remote valley to find the exact spot I stayed in.
 
Fifteen minutes gone. I still don't have the directions to my friends. The cushion I had is the reason I never used it. If I'd been running late, I'd have found the bar and left. Give me room and I'll wander into it.
 
I'm curious by nature. I love to discover things, and most days that pays off: I pick up bits from a dozen fields, I level up in places I never planned to. The same engine that makes me knowledgeable across a lot of subjects is the one that drove me into a valley in India when I was supposed to be finding a bar in my own city.
 
Curiosity points me everywhere. So I go everywhere. And "everywhere" is rarely where I said I was going.
 
Tony Robbins mentions this in *Giant Steps*: "if you want to achieve something, you must direct your mental and physical momentum toward your target. Where focus goes, energy flows". Look at the goal and you drift toward the goal. Look at everything else and you drift toward everything else. Your attention is the steering wheel. There's so much truth in these words, at least for me. 
 
Anyone who has ridden a bike knows the body version of this. Stare at the pothole and you hit the pothole. The trick every rider learns is to look at the gap, not the obstacle. Eyes lead. The wheels follow.
 
The reason this hit me is that I've felt the opposite, and it's night and day.
 
When I run, something narrows. My mind goes quiet and clean, and if I'm carrying a problem, all of me points at it. No bar names, no India, no tabs. Just the question, turning over, until ideas I didn't have at the start show up on their own. Same thing with pen and paper. No infinite library one click away. Just my own ideas and a place to put them down. The walls of the page are a feature. They keep me looking where I meant to look.
 
The screen does the opposite. It hands me a thousand exits, every one dressed up as discovery, and I take them, and I call it curiosity. It isn't. It's looking away from the goal and being surprised I didn't arrive.
 
This shows up at work too, just with a bigger bill. I've seen several teams where the product never ships because every nearby idea is more interesting than the one they were asked to build. On a personal level I've done career moves that left me empty because I was never clear on the destination, so any road looked fine. Each detour feels productive. Most of it is just me looking away from the goal and calling it research.
 
I'm not trying to kill the curiosity. It's one of the best things I've got. But there's a difference between curiosity I chose and curiosity that chose me, and the valley in India was the second kind.
 
So now, when it matters, I try to build the run. Close the tabs. Pick up the pen. Point myself at one thing and let the walls do their work.
 
Next time I'll find the bar first. Maybe?
